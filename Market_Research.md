The Incredible Hulk: Ultimate Destruction (Radical Entertainment, 2005) :

Hulk: Ultimate Destruction let players run wild as the Hulk, smashing buildings and vehicles in an open city. It became known as one of the [biggest “power fantasy”](https://www.space.com/best-marvel-games-of-all-time) games of its era. Critics and fans praised it as a top-notch take on super-powered destruction, and its free-roaming city smashing was revolutionary for 2005.
Hulk has all the abilities one would expect. He can pick up anything from lampposts to cars to tanks. He can jump massive distances. Best of all, thanks to the game's destructible environments, he can demolish large portions of the game's open-world city on a whim, just like the real Hulk would do. While there is a main story, side quests, and collectibles, the joy of The Incredible Hulk: Ultimate Destruction is in the name: it's the destruction.
it’s a cult classic that showed the appetite for playing a super-strong character causing destruction. The same studio later made Prototype, indicating Hulk UD’s power fantasy formula was strong enough to evolve. Its success (critically and in lasting fan memory) underscores the appeal of sandbox destruction and gives VillainMan a legacy to draw on (even though VillainMan will have a more arcade structure).


Saints Row 4 (Volition, 2013):

The Saints Row series wasn't initially designed to be a power fantasy; the first game was more or less a GTA clone with a few quirks to make it stand out. However, it struggled to compete with Rockstar's juggernaut franchise, so Volition decided to take Saints Row in a different, and more absurd, direction for its second, third, and fourth entries. While each game was solid in its own right, Saints Row 4 is the series at its most satirical, and as a result, it gives players an avatar with the most ridiculous abilities at their disposal.
From leaping over buildings to super-speed to elemental abilities and telekinesis, the "story" is really just an excuse to unleash one of the most absurdly powerful protagonists in video game history. Add to that the array of absurdist weapons, such as the Dubstep gun, and Saints Row 4 proves that a power fantasy doesn't need logic to be fun; in fact, throwing logic out the window may be the most fun decision of all.
Despite mixed critical reception at launch, Saints Row IV sold over [one million copies](https://www.vgchartz.com/game/226310/saints-row/) during its first week of release in August 2013, marking a significant commercial success for the franchise.
 While the game's initial launch sales reached this milestone, its lifetime sales figures have not been officially disclosed by the publisher.
While not focused on destruction, its comedic tone, freedom, and empowerment fantasy make it a clear inspiration for VillainMan.

Crackdown (Realtime Worlds, 2007):

Set in the fictional Pacific City, three districts are run by three distinct but equally dangerous gangs, overwhelming the local police force to the point of ineptitude. The only solution is to call in The Agency, who dispatch homegrown supersoldiers called Agents to clean up the streets. Along with the expected arsenal of vehicles and guns, players also have access to supersoldier abilities like enhanced speed, strength, and durability. 
Crackdown introduced players to a sandbox city where they could leap across skyscrapers and wreak havoc as a genetically enhanced super-cop. It became known for its addictive progression – gaining strength, speed, and explosive power – and its fully destructible environments (expanded in later entries). Crackdown sold over [1.5 million copies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crackdown_(video_game)). While Crackdown’s theme is heroic enforcement, its mechanics (empowering traversal, explosions, and large-scale chaos) aligns closely with VillainMan’s vision. VillainMan essentially flips Crackdown’s concept: instead of saving the city you destroy it. This inversion offers a fresh narrative hook while tapping into proven gameplay satisfaction loops.


Prototype (Radical Entertainment, 2009)

When the topic of power fantasy games comes up, Prototype is almost always mentioned. The shapeshifting sandbox gameplay remains unique in the industry to this day (apart from Prototype 2), and the blend of abilities—from sword arms to slingshot tentacles to mimicry stealth—is a veritable toybox of tools to cause as much chaos as possible. By the end of the game, once everything is unlocked, players are essentially unstoppable. Much like in GTA, causing havoc will bring in a retaliation force; in this case, the army.
Alex Mercer can go toe-to-toe with military helicopters, tanks, soldiers, and even other mutants. The beauty of Prototype is that engaging in the gameplay in this way, without worrying about plot or character, is endlessly fun, and many gamers keep Prototype game installed on their systems just to hop in and start a chaotic fight on occasion.
It was a commercial hit, [selling over 2.1 million copies worldwide by 2012.](https://prototype.fandom.com/wiki/Prototype)
Prototype proved that gamers relish the evil power fantasy (consuming people, blowing up tanks, and battling the military with superpowers). Its tone was serious and gory (unlike VillainMan’s comic vibe), but the core gameplay of causing chaos in a city and facing off against the military and monsters is similar.
